How to Start a Presentation
Don't start with a joke: Your audience isn't ready for it yet, and they are still settling in.

Make a Promise: Tell them exactly what they are going to learn or take away by the end of the talk. Give them a clear, immediate reason to stay and pay attention.

2. Core Heuristics (Rules of Engagement)
   Cycle Your Ideas: Repeat your core ideas multiple times. Repetition ensures your points are completely clear and helps cement the information.

Build a "Fence": Clearly distinguish your idea from someone else’s. Put a conceptual fence around your work so the audience knows exactly what your contribution is.

Use Verbal Punctuation: Periodically sum up the information you've covered. People's minds wander; verbal punctuation gives them a landmark to jump back on track.

Ask a Question: Ask an intriguing question and wait a few seconds for the audience to process it. It forces engagement and breaks the monotony.

3. Optimizing Time and Place
   Time: The optimal time for a lecture is 11:00 AM (people are awake, but not in a post-lunch slump).

Place: Ensure the room is well-lit so the audience can see you. Always inspect the room before your presentation. Ideally, pick a room size where the audience fills more than half the seats to maintain energy.

4. The Tools: Boards, Props, and Slides
   Whiteboards/Chalkboards: They inherently pace your presentation, give you a target to point at, and allow for dynamic graphics. Watch your hands—point at the board, but don't hold your hands behind your back.

Props: Use physical props to make your ideas visual. Visual perception is a highly effective way to interact with an audience and make a lasting emotional impact.

Slide Rules (Do's and Don'ts):

Less text, more images: Slides should reflect what you are saying, not the other way around.

Never read your slides: If you read your slides, the audience will just read ahead and ignore you.

Keep it simple: Remove clutter, distracting backgrounds, titles, and frames. Stop using bullet points.

Ditch the laser pointer: Using a laser pointer forces you to break eye contact with the audience. Instead, bake arrows directly into the slides to point things out.

5. "Getting Famous" (How to Make Ideas Memorable)
   If you want your ideas to be remembered and widely recognized, you need to utilize the "5 S's":

Symbols: Visuals associated with your ideas to attract attention.

Slogans: A short, memorable phrase describing your idea.

Surprise: Present a common fallacy that is no longer true to shock the audience into paying attention.

Salient Idea: An idea that sticks out and is easy to grasp.

Story: Package how you got to the idea into a compelling narrative.

6. How to End
   The Final Slide: Never put "Thank You," "The End," or "Q&A" on your final slide. It is a wasted opportunity. Your final slide should be a summary of your key contributions or the most important takeaway you want lingering on the screen.

Final Words: End with a strong concluding statement, a memorable thought, or a meaningful joke. Don't just trail off by saying, "Well, I guess that's it."
